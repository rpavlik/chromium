Chromium Introduction

Chromium is a new system for interactive rendering on clusters of workstations.  It is a completely extensible architecture, so new parallel rendering algorithms can be implemented on clusters with ease.  This release provides the ability to do parallel sort-first rendering in the style of WireGL.  Future releases and contributed modules will make Chromium much more flexible and powerful.

Even if you're completely happy with your WireGL setup, we recommend replacing it with Chromium for two reasons:

  1. Numerous bugs have been found and fixed since the last WireGL release
  2. The new configuration system will make setting up and reconfiguring your tiled display much simpler

This documentation is meant to serve as a guide for early adopters of the Chromium system.  The July release is an alpha release!  It will have many bugs in it, and certain features will not work.  However, Chromium cannot be a full success without the participation of the community, and early adoption of this technology is the best way to make an impact on the core design or modules early.

Up to this point, Chromium is mainly the product of one graduate student, Greg Humphreys.  However, a few other individuals deserve special mention.  Ian Buck wrote the original state tracking and bucketing system for WireGL, which the Chromium "tilesort" SPU and "state_tracker" library are loosely based on.  Ian has also been in charge of the WireGL release process and maintenance for our 80+ users.  Matthew Eldridge made significant improvements to WireGL's networking libraries and the overall system design, and his design philosophy (if not his code) lives on in Chromium.  Chris Niederauer did most of the implementation of the OpenGL extensions, including all of the NVIDIA specific ones.  He also did a lot of really useful physical labor for us last summer when we were building our cluster.
